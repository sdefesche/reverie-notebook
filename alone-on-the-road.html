ALONE ON THE ROAD

the notebook is the other player

Fifteen minutes, a bench, a pencil, two dice. How the whole game runs when it's just you — with a full example passage, played out line by line.

First

The Shape of Solo Play

Solo Reverie is a written conversation between three parties: you-the-traveler, who acts and notices; the oracle, who answers what you cannot decide; and the notebook, which remembers what you'd rather forget. There is no keeper. The tables, the dice, and the world outside your actual window hold the lantern together.

The loop is four verbs: write → ask → roll → write. Narrate in short lines, not paragraphs. When anything is genuinely open — what's behind the door, whether she believes you, why the bells rang — you ask, you roll, and you write the answer down before your cleverness can improve it. That last part is the entire discipline of solo play: the pencil moves before the editor wakes.

You are not writing a story. You are finding out what happens. The difference is whether the dice can still surprise you.

A solo passage runs 15–40 minutes and follows the same six beats as any passage: threshold, wander, fragment, crossing, echo, residue. The one addition: play it somewhere. A bench, a train, a café window. The environment is your third player, and the Threshold Moment will call on it.

Notation

A Solo Shorthand

Speed matters more than prose. A working notation, refined to five marks:

On the line

—  narration (what happens, what you do)
?  an oracle question, then the roll in brackets
✕  a crossing: facet, dice, result
«» anything the real world contributed
margin glyphs echo-marks, as always

Write questions as questions, always. "? Is the kiosk still open [4 — yes, but]" does more work than a paragraph, and six months from now it still reads as play, not diary.

Rules, Adjusted for One

Every Mechanic, Solo

Crossings

You roll both sides — traveler dice with one hand, World die with the other if you like the theater of it. Rulings about which facet leads are yours, but make them before rolling and write the stakes in one clause: "✕ Breath 2d — if I fail, she closes the shutter." Stakes written first can't be softened after.

On a Threshold Moment (tie): you succeed, you roll the Toll — and you look up from the notebook for ten honest seconds. The first real thing you notice enters the fiction now. Solo, this rule is at its most potent: there is no table to negotiate with, only you and whatever the world happens to be doing. Write it in «guillemets» so you can always find where reality leaked in.

The Oracle

Unchanged — except the interpretation rule. At a table, the player to your left reads the answer first; alone, you need a substitute for that outside voice. Use the margin rule: interpret every twist result (and… / but…) through your most recent echo-mark. If your last marked motif was rust, then "yes, but a price shows" has something to do with rust. This keeps the oracle's voice from becoming your own voice wearing a hat.

Riddles

Fully playable solo, and quietly the best solo module. Open a page, write the strange situation, and question it across the whole passage — or across several. The tally rules are identical: facts accumulate at roughly two per three questions, no-and crosses one out, seven facts and you must tell the truth using five — and every answer comes from the Oracle, since solo riddles have no poser and no direct answers. Solo advice: never draft the solution in your head while questioning. Ask, record, move on. Let the telling be genuinely assembled at the end from what stands on the page — you will surprise yourself, which is the point.

Echoes

Run the session-start roll exactly as written (1–2 last session, 3–4 any dog-eared page, 5–6 oldest unresolved mark). Solo, echoes carry extra weight because your notebook spans real geography — the marked page may have been written in another country. Honor the transformation rule strictly: same motif, different vessel. If it returned unchanged, it's a memory; changed, it's an omen.

Figures

Roll all three columns and let the Figure speak in written first lines: "— She says: the ferry doesn't take the morning crowd anymore." One line of dialogue per exchange is plenty. Their want is your compass for everything they do; their off-note goes straight into the margin.

Perils, the Road, Dreams

All as written. The Road's "everyone adds one detail" becomes: look out the actual window and write one detail of the actual landscape, made strange. Dream passages solo are a five-minute written exercise in the actual bed in the actual elsewhere — Dream leads, nothing weathers, Tolls bind, one thing crosses back.

In Practice

A Worked Passage

Below, a real-shaped solo passage: ~20 minutes, played on a station bench during an hour's delay. The traveler is Vesna — Bone 1, Breath 2, Dream 3 — whose keepsake is a broken compass, whose Burden is becoming someone who no longer stops, and whose margin already holds two glyphs: ⌘ rust and ≋ standing water.

Passage 9 · platform 2 · echo roll: 5 → oldest unresolved mark = ⌘ rust (p.14, "the rusted bell no one rings")
one breath. threshold: the departures board, but every destination is somewhere I've already been.
— Vesna waits for the slow train north. Third bench, the one the pigeons avoid.
? Is anyone else waiting for this train  [2 — no]
— empty platform, then. Which is wrong for a Friday.
? Do I know why it's empty  [3 — no, but a door opens]
— no… but the stationmaster's office door stands open. It is never open.
— inside: keys on a board, all rusted. one hook empty. (echo lands — rust again, new vessel)
fragment: rolled 43 — a meal set for four; three plates used. placing it here: on the office desk. riddle opened, p.22.
? RIDDLE: Were the eaters railway staff  [5 — yes] → fact 1: staff ate here.
? Recently  [6 — yes, and] → fact 2: recently — the tea still warm. and: the fourth plate is clean because it was never used, not because it was washed.
? Did the fourth person ever arrive  [1 — no, and it worsens] → cross out fact 1?? no — cross fact 2's comfort: the tea is warm because someone was here seconds ago.
— Vesna does not sit down.
✕ Dream 3d — listen for whoever just left; if I fail, they hear me first. rolled 4,2,6 vs World 6 — tie. Threshold Moment.
— toll [5]: someone learns my road-name.
«looked up: a man on the far platform wiping the same window over and over» — in the fiction: through the office window, a figure on the far platform, cleaning glass that is already clean, watching me in its reflection. standing water on the tiles at his feet.
? Does he answer to the empty key-hook  [4 — yes, but — read through last mark ≋: but the water answers first]
— the puddle at his feet ripples toward me. against the wind. Vesna leaves the office, unhurried, the way you leave a dog you don't trust.
— the slow train arrives, out of schedule and empty. she boards it.
residue: a clean fourth plate, and my road-name in someone's mouth I've never met. marked ⌘, dog-eared p.22 — riddle stands at 2 facts.

Notice what the machinery did there: the echo roll delivered rust before anything happened; the fragment table produced the passage's mystery; a single no, and turned a curiosity into dread; and the Threshold Moment pulled an actual stranger on an actual platform into the fiction, where he immediately became the most frightening thing in it. Total dice rolled: eight. Total prep: none.

Hard-Won

Advice

Keep the pencil ahead of the editor

Write stakes before rolling, questions before answers, answers before interpretations. Every one of these is a commitment device against the solo player's one enemy: retroactive authorship. If you catch yourself re-reading a line to improve it mid-passage, put the pencil down, look out the window, come back.

A question every three lines

The pacing rule that keeps solo play a game. Three narration lines without a roll means you are writing fiction, not discovering it. When in doubt, ask something you dread the answer to.

Notice; don't worldbuild

The temptation alone is to invent geography. Resist. Solo Reverie feeds on the world you are actually in: the real timetable, the real weather, the real stranger's real umbrella. Make the actual place strange rather than inventing a strange place. It's faster, and it's the game.

Let losses stand

Nobody is watching, which is exactly why the margin-damage rule matters solo. When the World die wins by 3+, mark both boxes and write what it looks like on Vesna's face. A solo campaign where nothing weathers is a daydream; the boxes are what make it a road.

Short passages, always closed

End every sitting with a residue line, even a five-minute one — especially one cut short by your actual train arriving. An unclosed passage goes stale; a residue line is a hook your next echo roll can bite. The interruption itself often makes the best residue.

One traveler, long roads

Solo play rewards staying with one traveler across many real journeys. Vesna in month six is a different instrument than Vesna in week one — her margin is thicker, her satchel has left things on three continents, and the echo roll has more past to weaponize. Waymarks, not dots, are her advancement.

Last

Back to the Table

Solo play is complete in itself, but it composts beautifully. Three ways your bench passages feed the shared game:

  • Postcards. Any solo residue qualifies. One image, one question, one mark — mail it to your table, and your delayed train becomes someone's opening scene.
  • Openings. A solo riddle left standing at four facts is a ready-made Opening: its threshold image, its fragment, and "the tally so far" as the tension. Hand the page to the group; they inherit the investigation.
  • Figures. Travelers you retire, strangers you rolled — copy their line into the shared notebook's cast. The man who wipes the clean window can be waiting at anyone's station now.
Played long enough, the solo notebook becomes the thing the whole game was designed toward: a fictional shadow of your actual life, thick with marks, waiting to echo.
