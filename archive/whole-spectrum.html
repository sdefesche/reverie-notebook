REVERIE

The Whole Spectrum

A table at home where a keeper fires beats into the dark. A book in the pocket that plays back your travels. Between them, a Station where everything you gather is filed — and nothing filed is ever lost.

The Thesis

Two faces, one game

Reverie already exists twice. There is the Hearth — the text-first virtual tabletop you built, where a keeper loads a prep sheet and fires beats to the players: narration, voices, visions, spheres of atmosphere, dice. And there is the Road — the offline archive designed for travel, where one to four wanderers play from a journal that remembers.

These are not two products. They are two postures of the same body: the Hearth is Reverie prepared; the Road is Reverie emergent. What unifies them is the Archive — a single deck of living cards that both faces read from and write to.

The Hinge

Spheres & the Station

Your own fiction already contains the join. The Hearth's atmosphere engine changes the whole mood of the table by invoking a sphere — the drowned moon's warm tavern light, the pale gate's cold liminality, moonside's silver unease. And your campaign world names the place between them: Placeholder Station, the hub where everything that exists must first be processed into existing.

So let it be literal, across the whole platform:

  • Spheres are places, moods, and planes at once. At the Hearth, invoking a sphere recolors the interface, the sound, the register of the storyteller's voice. On the Road, spheres are how the Archive files the atmosphere of real places.
  • The Lens files reality into the cosmology. Photograph a rain-dark alley and the on-device oracle reads its palette and light — and tells you which sphere this place lies in. Your actual journeys become journeys between spheres. The porous boundary now has a map.
  • The Station is the platform's own fiction. The Archive is a Station ledger. Cards are filings. Echoes are what the clerks of the Bureau send back, misdelivered on purpose. A traveler whose archive is empty is, strictly speaking, unprocessed — and character creation is the processing.

Why this matters

The Station gives the two faces a shared world without forcing one genre. A cozy hearth campaign and a solo passage on a night train both take place somewhere among the spheres — and anything filed in one can lawfully surface in the other. The bureaucracy-as-cosmos joke is also the data model.

Face One · exists today

The Hearth

The virtual tabletop as built: a keeper's private prep sheet in plain Markdown, with tagged blocks that can be fired to the table. Prose between blocks belongs to the keeper alone. This design is already right — the enrichment is what flows into it and out of it, not a rebuild.

What the Hearth gains from the merger

  • Live cards. Anything that happens at the table — a named stranger, an unexplained detail, a phrase that lands — can be captured as an Archive card mid-session, motif-tagged with one tap.
  • The Archivist as whisperer. At the Hearth the narrating intelligence never speaks to players. It sits at the keeper's shoulder: proposing the next beat, recalling that the rosemary motif last appeared four passages ago, drafting an @npc line in a voice already established.
  • Echoes at the table. The same Echo engine that haunts the Road can offer the keeper a resurfaced card as an optional beat — the Hearth's version of the world remembering.
Face Two · the portable practice

The Road

The Wayfarer's Archive, in brief — the full treatment stands in its own reference. An installable, offline-first journal that contains a game engine; one to four travelers; no permanent keeper required.

The Traveler

Made in ten minutes from five answers — road-name, why you wander, what you carry, what you fear becoming, the place that appears in your dreams — each answer becoming a card the engine begins working on immediately. Six dots across three facets:

Bone
endurance · force · the body's memory
Breath
presence · nerve · meeting the world
Dream
symbol-sight · intuition · the undertow

The Crossing

Resolution in one breath

Traveler d6s (dice = leading facet, keep highest) vs one World d6.
Higher — passage. Tie — the Toll: you pass, the world asks a question, something small is owed. Lower — turned aside; mark Weathering if danger.
Doubles — Turn of Weather: everyone looks up; the first real thing noticed enters the fiction.

No hit points: three weathering boxes per facet. All facets closed means the Long Rest — a full real-world day away from the story. Travelers retire into the world's cast; they are never spent like ammunition.

The Undertow, in layers

  • Layer 0 — the die, always: graded yes/no on a d6, interpreted together.
  • Layer 1 — the Lens, offline: the camera reads palette, light, texture, and hour on-device and folds them through seeded tables into omens — and into sphere filings.
  • Layer 2 — the Archivist, when signal is found: the book speaking back, having read everything. When the signal dies mid-sentence, the sentence stays unfinished.

Memory as mechanism

Fragments open threads without hidden answers — the resonant interpretation becomes canon. Motifs are recurring symbols tracked as first-class objects. Echoes resurface old cards transformed, on a spaced schedule — one passage, three passages, one journey, one year — so that across time the Archive becomes a fictional shadow of a real life.

The Passage

  1. Threshold — one vivid image opens.
  2. Wander — conversation, travel, atmosphere.
  3. Fragment — something unexplained appears.
  4. Crossing — someone risks what matters.
  5. Echo — an old card returns, changed.
  6. Residue — an unresolved image, filed as the next seed.

Modes: solo with the book keeping the lantern; duo with custody alternating each scene; a band of three or four with the lantern rotating each passage — the keeper is never the one person who knows.

The Signature

The Round Trip

The single feature that makes the spectrum one product: cards and beats are convertible currency, and the exchange runs both directions through the Station.

Road → Hearth: the compiler

At the end of a journey, the Archive can compile a prep sheet. Everything you gathered wandering — solo passages on trains, vessels photographed on beaches, strangers half-invented in hostel bars — is arranged into a fireable Markdown file for your Sunday table:

Archive cardBecomes at the Hearth
Waypoint@atmosphere (its sphere) + @narration of its mood
Vessel photo@image with its charge as the caption
Figure@npc, voice notes carried over
Fragment@note or @story, thread left open for the table
Residue image@story — the threshold of a scene
Crossing@roll / @prompt at the tense point

You return from three weeks of travel with a campaign arc no one wrote at a desk. The keeper's private prose between beats stays yours to add — the compiler drafts the skeleton, never the soul.

Hearth → Road: the write-back

Every fired beat and its outcome is filed as a Passage card. Strangers voiced at the table become Figures; details that landed become Fragments; everything is motif-taggable in the moment. Then the Echo engine owns it all — and three months later, alone on a platform in another country, the Road offers you back the phrase an NPC spoke at your own table, transformed, as graffiti on a wall.

The people at your Sunday table and the roads you walk alone begin, slowly, to inhabit the same dream.
Systems

The System Bridge

The Hearth speaks Troika! today — 2d6, roll under skill. The Road speaks the Crossing. Rather than force a migration, the platform is system-light with the Crossing as its native tongue and Troika! as a fully supported engine, joined by a clean mapping:

The correspondence

Bone ↔ Stamina — the body's reserve
Breath ↔ Skill — meeting the world capably
Dream ↔ Luck — the current beneath events

A Troika! test and a Crossing led by the corresponding facet are treated as the same fictional event. Legacy prep sheets — Form 7-Theta, the plane-hopping runs — fire unchanged. New sheets may declare either engine in their front matter; mixed tables simply agree on one per campaign.

Characters convert once, at the Station, and hold dual papers thereafter: a Troika! sheet for the Hearth, facets for the Road, one identity in the Archive. The Turn of Weather rule applies under both engines — doubles on any test invite the real world in.

Technical Shape

One Archive, Two Shells

A single card store is the source of truth. The two faces are shells around it.

  • The shared spine: every entity — traveler, vessel, fragment, motif, waypoint, figure, passage, journey, prep sheet, beat — is a card with motif links. Beats are cards with a broadcast payload; prep sheets are ordered collections of them.
  • Road shell: offline-first PWA; IndexedDB; service worker; on-device Lens; local Echo scheduler; installable and complete without any server, forever.
  • Hearth shell: the existing online app — rooms, real-time broadcast, spheres, sound — reading prep sheets from the same card format and writing passages back.
  • Sync at the Station: when the Road finds signal, archives reconcile — last-write-wins per card, motifs merged, echo schedules preserved. Two travelers parting ways exchange cards by QR, no server needed.
  • The Archivist: one narrating intelligence, two registers — the book's voice on the Road, the whisper at the keeper's shoulder at the Hearth. Prompted from a distilled archive digest either way.
  • Common ground: the whole Archive exports as a Markdown vault, one file per card, motif links as wikilinks — at home in an Obsidian vault, and a prep sheet is just a view over it.
