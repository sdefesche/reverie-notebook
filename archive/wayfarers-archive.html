REVERIE

The Wayfarer's Archive

One traveler to four. Any road, any table, any bench on any platform. No signal required — but the oracle deepens where signal is found.

The Thesis

What this is now

Reverie began as a manual: a contemplative travel RPG played at a table. This document defines its second life — a portable practice carried in the pocket, played by one wanderer or a small band, where the journal is the game and the world outside the window is a legitimate rulebook.

Three things distinguish it from everything it grew out of:

  • The Archive is the engine. There is no character sheet separate from the journal. Every entry — object, place, dream, stranger — is a living card, and the system plays those cards back to you, transformed, across sessions, journeys, and years.
  • The world is a component. Real weather, real objects, real photographs, and real coincidence are mechanically invited into play — not as flavor, but through explicit procedures.
  • The oracle is layered, never gated. Dice always work. The camera oracle works offline. The Archivist — the narrating intelligence — awakens when connectivity allows, and its absence is part of the fiction: the oracle grows stranger near the towers, quieter in the hills.
This is not a lighter fantasy game. It is a walking meditation wearing the clothes of one.
Design Law

The Three Vows

Every rule, screen, and line of code answers to these. If a feature breaks a vow, the feature goes.

I · The World Is Porous

Reality may enter the fiction at any moment, by procedure. A receipt, a downpour, an overheard phrase — the system provides doors, not walls. Digital tools must amplify this porousness, never replace it with a screen.

II · The Archive Is the Game

Play produces memory; memory produces play. Nothing written is ever wasted — the system owes every card a second life. Losing the archive is the only true character death.

III · The Oracle Deepens, Never Gates

Every question answerable online must be answerable offline, more plainly. Connectivity adds voice and depth; it never unlocks the door. A traveler with one die and a notebook has the full game.

Character

The Traveler

A traveler is made in ten minutes, from five answers and six dots. There are no classes, no builds, no advancement ladder — travelers change by accumulating history, not power.

Five Answers

  • What are you called on the road?
  • Why did you begin wandering? (your first Thread — an open mystery)
  • What do you always carry? (your first Vessel — photograph it into the Archive)
  • What do you fear becoming? (your Burden — it occupies a satchel slot, always)
  • What place still appears in your dreams? (your first Motif — the Echo engine's first seed)

Notice: character creation is archive creation. The five answers become five cards, and the engine begins working on them immediately.

Three Facets

Distribute six dots, no facet above three, none at zero.

Bone
endurance · force · the body's memory
Breath
presence · nerve · meeting the world
Dream
symbol-sight · intuition · the undertow

The Satchel

Seven slots. Objects fill slots — but so do heavy memories, unkept promises, and fears. To take on something new and weighty, you may have to set something down, and say aloud where you left it. What is set down becomes a card. The Echo engine remembers where you left it.

Resolution

The Crossing

Most of play is conversation: describe, ask, notice, decide. Dice appear only at a Crossing — when the outcome is uncertain and something would be lost by failing. Everything else simply happens.

The Roll

Roll Traveler dice (d6s equal to your leading facet, keep highest)
against one World die (d6).

Traveler higher — passage. It goes as you intended.
Tie — the Toll. You pass, but the world asks a question of you (draw from the Undertow) and something small is owed.
World higher — turned aside. The world moves instead; if there was danger, mark Weathering on the leading facet.

Turn of Weather

If the Traveler and World dice show doubles, the real world is invited in: everyone looks up from the table. The first thing anyone notices — a sound, a passerby, the light changing — enters the fiction immediately, interpreted together. This is the porous boundary, mechanized.

Weathering

There is no hit-point track. Each facet has three weathering boxes. A fully weathered facet closes: it cannot lead a Crossing, and the traveler shows it — a limp, a silence, a distance in the eyes.

When all three facets close, the traveler must take the Long Rest: they leave the story until a full real-world day has passed. No death — travelers are not spent like ammunition. But absence has weight, and the story moves without them.

Weathering clears one box per facet at each genuine rest: a night's sleep, a shared meal, a real pause in the real journey.

Oracle

The Undertow

When no one at the table knows the answer — including whoever is keeping the lantern — ask the Undertow. It has three layers, and the deeper layers are luxuries, never requirements.

Layer 0 · The Die — always

Ask a yes/no question, roll a d6, interpret together:

RollThe water says
1No — and it worsens
2No
3No — but a door opens
4Yes — but a price shows
5Yes
6Yes — and it deepens

Layer 1 · The Lens — offline, on-device

Point the camera at anything: a found object, a doorway, a stain on a wall. The device reads what it can without any network — dominant colors, brightness, texture, time of day, weather if cached — and folds those readings through seeded prompt tables into an omen. The same object photographed at dusk and at noon yields different omens. Nothing leaves the device.

Layer 2 · The Archivist — when signal is found

The narrating intelligence. It has read the whole Archive. It answers in the voice of the journal itself — never as a chatbot, always as the book speaking back. It proposes Fragments, notices Motifs the table has missed, gives found objects histories, and can carry a full solo session. When the signal dies mid-sentence, the sentence stays unfinished. That, too, is an omen.

Memory

Fragments, Motifs, Echoes

Fragments

A Fragment is an unexplained detail, presented without its solution — every clock showing a different hour, warm tea in an abandoned chapel. Fragments open Threads. Threads do not have hidden answers waiting to be found; the truth is discovered through play, and the most resonant interpretation at the table becomes canon.

Motifs

A Motif is a recurring symbol the Archive tracks as a first-class object: rosemary, drowned bells, the number nine, a woman in grey. Any card may be tagged with motifs. Motifs are how the Archive dreams.

The Echo Engine

The signature system. Old cards resurface, transformed — and the schedule is borrowed from how human memory actually works:

Narrative Spaced Repetition

A card becomes eligible to echo after:
1 passage → then 3 passages → then 1 journey → then 1 year.

When eligible, the engine offers it back changed: same motif, different vessel. The scent from the inn returns at a ruined shrine. The stranger's phrase returns as graffiti in another country.

Offline, echoes are chosen by motif co-occurrence and the schedule. Online, the Archivist chooses with more cunning — but the traveler never knows which mind chose. That uncertainty is deliberate.

Across years of travel, the Archive becomes a fictional shadow of a real life — a second country you have been walking through all along.
Found Objects

Vessels

A Vessel is a real object admitted into the fiction: a shell, a ticket stub, a key found on a beach. The procedure:

  • Find it in the actual world.
  • Photograph it into the Archive — the Lens reads it and offers a Charge.
  • Name it together: what it is in the fiction, who held it, what it wants.
  • Carry it — a Vessel occupies a satchel slot in the game and a pocket in reality.

A Charge is a single latent property, phrased as a hedge: "it grows cold near untruth," "it hums where someone drowned." Charges are never triggered by the carrier — only by the world, at the lantern-keeper's or the oracle's whim.

Play

Passages & Modes

A session is a Passage. Whether it lasts twenty minutes on a delayed train or three hours at a hostel table, it moves through the same six beats:

  1. Threshold — a single vivid image opens the passage. On the road, it may simply be described from the window.
  2. Wander — conversation, travel, atmosphere. Questions over answers.
  3. Fragment — something unexplained appears, from the deck, the oracle, or a player.
  4. Crossing — someone risks something that matters.
  5. Echo — the engine offers an old card back, transformed. The table decides how it manifests.
  6. Residue — the passage closes on an unresolved image, written into the Archive as the next passage's seed. Not closure. Resonance.

Solo — the traveler and the book

The Archive keeps the lantern. The Undertow answers questions; the Archivist, when awake, narrates. Solo play leans journal-first: write, ask, roll, write. A passage can be played entirely on a park bench in fifteen minutes.

Duo — alternating custody

Two travelers trade the lantern each scene. Whoever holds it frames the world and voices its figures; the other acts. The oracle arbitrates what neither should decide. Ideal for a traveling pair — one narrates the town you have both just walked through.

Table — three or four, rotating keeper

No permanent GM. Each passage, the lantern passes to the next keeper, who prepares only three things: an opening image, one Fragment, one tension. The truth of every mystery is discovered at the table, so the keeper is never the one person who knows. Everyone plays a traveler; the keeper's traveler simply stands quieter that passage.

Ritual brackets

Every passage opens and closes the same way, in every mode: one breath of actual silence, then the threshold image; and at the end, each participant names one image they will remember. The Archive records the named images — they are prime Echo material.

Platform

The Archive — Technical Shape

An installable, offline-first progressive web app. No app store, no account wall, no dependency on any server to play. It is a journal that happens to contain a game engine.

Architecture

  • Shell: PWA — service worker precache, installable to home screen, fully functional in airplane mode from first install onward.
  • Store: IndexedDB as the single source of truth; the archive lives on the device, encrypted export available.
  • Lens (L1): on-device canvas analysis of photographs — palette, luminance, texture entropy, timestamp — folded through seeded oracle tables. No image ever needs to leave the device.
  • Archivist (L2): LLM narrator via API when online, prompted with a distilled archive digest plus the live passage. Graceful degradation is a design feature, not an apology.
  • Echo scheduler: local — motif index, co-occurrence weights, spaced-repetition intervals; runs identically offline and on.
  • Context seeds: optional geolocation and cached weather feed the Lens and the threshold-image generator. Every sensor is opt-in per journey.
  • Shared table: one device passed hand to hand — the device is the lantern. Cards trade between two archives by QR when travelers part ways.
  • Export: the entire archive renders to a Markdown vault — one file per card, motif links as wikilinks — dropping cleanly into an Obsidian vault. The Archive is never a silo.
