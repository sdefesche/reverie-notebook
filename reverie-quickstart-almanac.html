The One New Rule

The Right Tool

Rule

If your gear plainly fits the attempt — rope for the climb, iron filings for the ghost — roll one extra Traveler die.
Maximum four dice, still keep highest. One item per roll.

That's the entire equipment system. No bonuses, no stats — an item either fits or it doesn't, and the table judges "plainly" out loud. Since the satchel holds seven slots and the Burden takes one, what you packed is your build. The player who brought chalk to a haunted mill made a character choice; honor it.

Variant for a slower table: the extra die only comes from gear bought, found, or bargained for in play — making the outfitter's shop a real scene. For a first hunt, skip the variant: let packing be free and clever.

Arms

Weapons — traits, not damage

Rule

A weapon is slots + permission to do harm + one trait.
The trait is always true, for better and worse.
Against a Peril, weapons only bite after the Telling — before it, they buy escape at best.

No damage dice: a Crossing with a weapon does what the fiction says — drives off, wounds, pins, finishes — shaped by the facts you've established. The trait is the interesting part, and it cuts both ways: loud wins fights and wakes things; borrowed must go back, and its owner will ask what it did.

Sturdy things — the only armor rule

A heavy coat, a stout shield, a thick door held right: once per passage, a sturdy thing you carry or grab may absorb one weathering mark — and break. Mended at a real rest with a scene or a Crossing. Things save you by being ruined; that's what things are for.

Assembling the hunt

1. Roll village (5d6) + monster (4d6) + two rumors.
2. Pick three locations where its sign shows strongest.
3. Give one Figure a reason to lie about it.
4. Write the default truth in two sentences from the seed.
5. Draw both tracks where everyone can see. Hunt.
