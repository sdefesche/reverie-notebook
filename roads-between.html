THE ROADS BETWEEN

Travel, strangers, dangers, dreams — and play by post

Six modules that plug into the core game. Each is optional; each uses only the notebook, the dice, and the mail.

Module One

The Road

Use this whenever getting there matters — a day's walk, a night train, a ferry leg. One roll per leg, so a long journey is three or four legs, not thirty.

Procedure — one leg of travel

  1. Each traveler adds one detail. What changed in the landscape? What sign of life remains? Around the table, one sentence each.
  2. The lead traveler rolls the World d6 on the Road table below.
  3. Play what comes. A road event is a scene, not a paragraph — but it can be a short one.
d6The road brings
1A Peril stirs — advance one active Peril, or introduce one.
2A Figure on the road — roll on the Figure tables.
3A find — describe an object here; it may become a Vessel.
4The weather turns — the actual weather, now, enters the fiction.
5An omen — roll d66 on the Fragment table; it needn't resolve here.
6Good road — arrive early; each traveler erases one weathering box or learns one rumor about the destination (say it aloud, write it down).

Draw the journey as you go: a point-to-point line map across a notebook spread — dots for places, a line for each leg, one word of atmosphere beside each dot. This map is the campaign.

Module Two

Figures

A Figure is anyone the road provides. Three rolls make one — face, want, and the off-note. The off-note is what makes them Reverie's: every stranger carries one small wrongness, which may mean nothing, or everything.

Give every named Figure a line in the notebook. If their off-note ever recurs, mark it in the margin — Figures are echo material of the first order. A retired traveler enters this cast permanently and may be rolled instead of a stranger.

Module Three

Perils

Reverie has no monster stats. A Peril is any patient danger: a flood rising, a rumor spreading, a debt collector gaining ground, something following at exactly the same distance. Perils give the game teeth without combat.

Procedure

  1. Name it and draw three boxes at the top of a notebook page. Perils are always named aloud — dangers in Reverie are personal.
  2. It advances — mark one box — whenever: a Crossing against it fails; the Toll table feeds it (the table's choice); or the oracle answers No, and… to a question about safety. An urgent Peril feeds on every Toll, and arrives in roughly four scenes instead of six — declare urgency when you name it.
  3. At three marks it arrives. Frame the scene where it can no longer be avoided. Now only Crossings, cost, or cleverness remain.
  4. It is undone by understanding. Every Peril is also a Riddle: establish seven facts about it, and the truth-telling includes how it can be ended, escaped, or appeased. You can outrun a flood; you can only riddle a curse.

Run at most two Perils at once. A Peril that arrives and passes leaves a mark on the map — write what it changed.

Module Four · play by post

Passage Postcards

The travel game that travels. When the table is apart — one of you abroad, the campaign on pause — the game continues by mail. A real postcard, stamped and sent, is a game move.

Sending

On the back of a postcard, write exactly three things:
1. one residue image from a passage you played (solo counts)
2. one open question you cannot answer
3. one echo-mark symbol, drawn where the stamp faces it

Sign it with your road-name. Mail it.

Receiving

A received postcard is binding, gently:
— your next passage must open with its image as the Threshold
— its question goes to the Oracle in that passage
— its symbol enters your margin; the motif is now shared

Tape the postcard into the notebook. It is a Vessel.

Solo travelers feeding a home table, two campaigns cross-pollinating, a duo separated for a season — all the same procedure. Nothing needs to be synchronized; the mail's delay is the mechanic. An image sent from a night train in one country arrives transformed by three weeks into someone's opening scene in another.

Design the postcard back as a true postal back: message left, address right — and the oracle table printed inside the stamp box.
Module Five

Dream Passages

At any genuine rest, one traveler may dream. A Dream Passage is ten minutes, played by different weather:

  • Dream leads everything. All Crossings use the Dream facet, whatever is attempted. Strength means nothing here.
  • Nothing weathers. No boxes are marked in dreams — but Tolls still bind. What you owe in a dream, you owe.
  • Everything is symbol. People appear as their off-notes. Places appear as their feelings. The Fragment table is read as imagery, never event.
  • One thing crosses back. When the dreamer wakes, the table chooses one object, phrase, or image from the dream. Write it, mark it. It must appear in waking play before the journey ends — that is the dream's demand.

Dreams are the game's echo factory and its quietest mode — a good Dream Passage is mostly questions. Solo, it is a five-minute written exercise before sleep, in the actual bed, in the actual elsewhere.

Module Six · play tonight

Six Openings

A ready Opening is a keeper's whole preparation: image, fragment, tension. Pick one or roll d6, read it aloud, begin. Everything else is discovered.

1 · The Ferry That Waits
ImageA ferry moored at dawn; the ferryman asleep standing up, hand on the rope.
FragmentHe refuses payment from one of you — only one.
TensionThe far shore is on nobody's map, and the queue behind you is growing.
2 · The Counting Town
ImageChalk marks on every door, and every mark is one lower than yesterday's.
FragmentBells at the wrong hour; nobody reacts.
TensionThe count reaches zero tomorrow, and the only person who'll discuss it is packing.
3 · The Inn of Returned Things
ImageAn inn whose lost-and-found shelf holds something a traveler once set down, far from here.
FragmentThe innkeeper knows your order before you speak.
TensionThe returned thing wants to go back to where it was left — and it has learned patience.
4 · The Backwards River
ImageThe river running the wrong way, calm as ever; boats queuing at the mouth, unwilling to enter.
FragmentA tune you know, whistled back at you from underground.
TensionSomething upstream is drinking, and the wells here have started tasting of elsewhere.
5 · The Unsent Letter
ImageIn a stranger's shop, a letter addressed to one of your road-names — postmarked next month.
FragmentYour own handwriting, somewhere you have never been.
TensionThe sender is expected in town before you plan to leave.
6 · The Two Bridges
ImageThe old bridge stands new; the new bridge stands ancient. Locals use neither after dark.
FragmentFootprints leading onto one bridge; none leading off.
TensionThe toll-keeper accepts only memories, and gives honest change.
