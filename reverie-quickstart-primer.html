Reverie Quickstart

THE HUNTER'S PRIMER

for players who know their way around a d20
The Pitch

What kind of game is this?

You're monster hunters. But in Reverie, you can't kill what you don't understand. Every monster, curse, and haunting has a weakness — and the weakness is hidden behind facts you have to hunt down first. Ask questions, search places, chase leads, take risks. When you've learned enough, you get to name the monster's weakness yourselves — and then use it in the showdown.

Think Witcher contracts: research first, fight smart, win because you figured it out. Swinging a sword at something you don't understand mostly gets you hurt.

Clever beats strong. Every time.
Your Hunter

Three stats. That's all.

StatCoversIn D&D terms
BONEstrength, toughness, climbing, holding on, breaking throughSTR + CON
BREATHtalking, bluffing, sneaking, quick reactions, nerveDEX + CHA
DREAMnoticing, gut feelings, weird senses, spotting what's offWIS + all your perception checks

You get 6 points across the three, minimum 1, maximum 3. A 3 means you're great at it. A 1 means that's where trouble finds you — and trouble will find it.

You also carry a satchel with 7 slots. What you carry is what you have — no shopping mid-hunt, so pack smart and improvise hard. Rope is always better than you think.

Rolling

The Crossing — one roll, everything

No attack rolls, no AC, no skill list. When you try something risky, it's a Crossing:

How to roll

Say what you do and which stat it uses.
Roll d6s equal to that stat — keep the highest.
The GM rolls one World die (d6).

You're higher → you did it. Clean.
You're lower → the world moves instead — and if it was dangerous, you take a hit.
TIE → the best result in the game. Read on.

The tie — the Threshold Moment

On a tie, three things happen at once: you succeed, you owe something (the GM rolls what — maybe you drop gear, maybe something learns your name), and the world leans in: everyone looks up from the table for ten seconds, and the first real thing anyone notices — a sound in the house, headlights through the window — enters the game right now. The room you're playing in is part of the game. Yes, really.

Damage

Getting hurt — Weathering

No hit points. Each stat has 3 damage boxes. Fail a dangerous Crossing and you mark one on the stat you used — two if the World die beat you by 3 or more (that's a critical against you). Every mark shows: a limp, shaking hands, missing the obvious clue.

  • Three marks on one stat = it's closed. You can't lead with it until you heal.
  • All three stats closed = you're out of the hunt — carried to safety, back next adventure. Nobody dies. But your friends finish this one without you, so don't get reckless.
  • A real rest in the fiction (safe place, food, night) heals one box, your choice. Healing is slow. Plan around it.
Investigation

Hunting the monster — the Fact Tally

Here's the engine of every hunt. The monster is a mystery with a hidden weakness. You crack it by asking yes/no questions — of villagers, of clues, of the Oracle (a d6 the whole table gets to interpret):

1No — and it gets worse
2No
3No — but you find another way in
4Yes — but there's a catch
5Yes
6Yes — and there's more

Every clear answer becomes a fact, written on the hunt page. A "No, and…" is the mystery fighting back — cross out one fact you thought you had. When 7 facts stand, anyone at the table can attempt the Telling: explain what the monster really is and how to beat it, using at least 5 of the facts. If the table buys it — that becomes true. You didn't find the weakness. You built it. Now go use it.

The Enemy

Monsters are Perils

A monster doesn't have HP either. It has a name and 3 threat boxes. Every time you fail against it, waste time, or push your luck, it gets a mark — and at three marks it comes for you, on its terms, showdown time, ready or not. So the whole hunt is a race: your 7 facts against its 3 marks.

In the showdown, fighting works like everything else — Crossings — but if you've done the Telling, you know its weakness, and clever plans that use it just work, or roll with serious advantage. Hunters who skipped the homework fight something they don't understand. Good luck with that.

Loot

Treasure — Vessels

Magic items exist, and they're strange: a bell that rings underwater, a key without a lock, a compass that points at something lost. Each has one hidden Charge — a power that fires when the world decides, never when you want it to. Half the fun is figuring out what your loot actually does. Real objects from the real table can become Vessels too: the GM will show you how.

One Last Thing

How to be good at this game

  • Ask questions. Questions are ammunition. The player who asks "why are all the dogs silent?" is doing more damage than any sword.
  • Say what you do, not what you roll. "I wedge the door with my boot and listen" beats "I roll perception." The GM tells you if dice are even needed — most clever things just work.
  • Running away is a power move. The monster gets stronger every time you fail. Retreat, heal, come back with facts.
  • Use your 1. Your weak stat is where your best scenes happen. The tough guy facing the ghost is the story.
Learn it. Name it. Beat it.
