JOURNEYS

a journey is a riddle you walk

Passages are the sessions; journeys are the stories. This chapter defines how they begin, what holds them together, how they end — and what a table does in its very first hour.

Definition

What a Journey Is

A journey is a named arc of passages, held together by three things written on its first page: a name, an anchor, and a question. Everything a traveler becomes, they become at journeys' ends — Waymarks resolve there, Burdens are replaced there, retirements happen there. The journey is the unit of change.

The anchor — what shape it takes

Choose one when the journey begins. The anchor decides how the journey ends, so choose honestly:

The DestinationA fictional goal: reach the drowned city, return the coin, find the sender. Ends on arrival — or on turning away, which counts.
The TripBracketed by a real journey: this holiday, this posting, this month abroad. Begins when you leave; ends when you're home. The strongest anchor for solo and duo play.
The SeasonA fixed span of passages — six, unless agreed otherwise. Ends when the count does, arrived or not. The reliable anchor for a table that meets on Sundays.

The question — what the journey asks

Every journey opens one question it cannot yet answer, written under the name: Why does the coin want to go back? What is emptying the harbor towns? What are we to each other when the road runs out? The question is a slow riddle: it keeps a fact tally like any riddle, but its facts accumulate across passages, fed by fragments, figures, and echoes. It is answered — or deliberately left standing — only at the journey's end.

A journey with no question is a commute. The question is what the walking is for.
Session Zero

The First Hour

A new campaign's first hour is its own small ritual — travelers, table, and boundary, in that order. Solo travelers do a quiet version of the same four steps.

  1. Make travelers together. The five answers and six dots, aloud, in turns — each answer heard by everyone becomes shared material. Then lay the sheets side by side and look at the 1s: each traveler's weak facet is where their crises will live. Different 1s means every kind of trouble belongs to someone. Notice; don't enforce.
  2. Bind the band. One question, answered once, together: "What do you share besides the road?" A debt, a funeral, a rumor you all heard, a door you all saw close. One sentence, written at the top of the journey page. This is the band's founding fact — the first entry in the journey's slow riddle.
  3. Set the porous dial. Decide, out loud, how much of the real world this journey admits (next card). This is a comfort setting as much as an aesthetic one, and it can be re-set between journeys.
  4. Agree the lantern can be dimmed. Say it once, plainly: anyone may pause, redirect, or fade a scene to black, any time, no explanation owed — and a Burden is always the traveler's fear, written at whatever distance from the player's own feels right, rewritable between passages without ceremony.

The porous dial

SealedThe fiction stays fiction. Threshold Moments draw from imagined surroundings; no found objects required. For indoor tables and heavy nights.
AjarThe default. Threshold Moments and Vessels admit the real world; the rest is invented. Reality knocks; the table answers.
OpenFull bleed: real weather, real walks, the Duet, world-oracles, doors passing the lantern at real doorways. For traveling pairs and brave tables.

Then play the first passage immediately, even a short one — pick an Opening or use the real room's window. A first hour that ends with a residue image has begun a campaign; one that ends with only sheets has held a meeting.

For every journey after the first, one rule binds beginnings — the chain rule at journey scale: the new journey's question or opening image must transform something left standing by the last — an unresolved riddle, an uncollected Toll, a thing set down in a margin. Campaigns are braided, never restarted.

On the Road

Traveling

A journey's middle is passages, legs, and rests — all already defined. What the journey layer adds is rhythm and bookkeeping, kept deliberately thin:

  • The map grows one dot per place, one line per leg, one word of atmosphere per dot. Perils that arrive leave their mark on it. The map is the campaign's memory of where; the margins remember what.
  • The tally grows when the fiction earns it. When a passage surfaces something true about the journey's question, write it as a fact on the journey page — same rules as any riddle, including no, and… crossing one out. Most passages add nothing. That's correct; slow riddles are slow.
  • Tolls are dog-eared. Every spoken Toll — every promise the world will collect — gets its page folded. The echo roll is the collection agency: when a Toll's page comes up, the debt returns, transformed, and this passage is when the world collects.
  • Mid-journey is dream country. Dream Passages and postcards belong to the middle third, when the question has facts but no shape. A dream may add a tally fact directly if the table agrees it was true.
Arrival

Ending a Journey — the Last Passage

When the anchor closes — the destination reached or refused, the trip's return ticket used, the season's count spent — the next passage is played as the Last Passage. It follows the six beats with three rites folded in:

  1. The litany. At the threshold, before the opening image, read the journey's margins aloud — every glyph, in order, as a litany. Five minutes of the campaign performing its own memory. The opening image must then transform one thing from the litany.
  2. The telling, or the standing. In place of the fragment beat, the journey's question comes due. If seven facts stand on the journey page, the truth may be told — the full riddle rite, five facts woven in, one sentence each from the others. If fewer stand, the table chooses: force a telling with what stands (the valve), or let it stand — written across the journey page, unanswered, and eligible to seed a future journey. Standing is not failure. Some questions are load-bearing.
  3. Waymark resolution. After the residue, each traveler resolves the journey — exactly one: move one facet dot (the archetype shifts; say what changed on the road to change them) · replace the Burden with a fear earned on this journey · or retire — the traveler enters the world's cast as a Figure, and the player makes someone new before the next journey begins.

Endings the anchor didn't choose

Journeys also end by abandonment — the table walks away from the question — and that is played, not skipped: a Last Passage still happens, the litany still read, the question marked abandoned rather than standing. Abandoned questions echo hardest. The one ending Reverie doesn't have is the unmarked stop: if the campaign is truly over, give it a Last Passage anyway, even a fifteen-minute one. The notebook deserves a spine, not a fade.

Between

The Interval

Between journeys, real time passes — days or months, and the game is better for not pretending otherwise. Three things happen in the interval, none requiring a session:

  • Everything unresolved survives. Standing questions, dog-eared Tolls, half-tallied riddles, Figures with unmet wants — nothing closes by default. The next journey's chain rule will reach back into this pile.
  • Travelers weather nothing and heal everything. All boxes clear in an interval. The road is hard; between roads is life.
  • Postcards travel. The interval is postcard season — solo passages played on real trips feed the next journey's opening, and a band scattered across a summer reconvenes to find its mail already in the notebook.
A campaign, in the end, is a shelf of journeys in one thickening notebook — each opened by transforming what the last one left standing. The first journey begins from nothing. No other journey ever does.
